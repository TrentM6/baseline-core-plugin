# Stakeholder Management

> Frameworks for navigating organizational dynamics — mapping stakeholders, building buy-in, and influencing without authority.

**Part of:** [Strategic Advisory](../strategic-advisory-skill.md)

## Stakeholder Analysis Matrix

### Power/Interest Grid

Map every stakeholder on two dimensions:

```
High Power
      |
  MANAGE CLOSELY        KEEP SATISFIED
  (High Power,          (High Power,
   High Interest)        Low Interest)
      |
      |
  KEEP INFORMED         MONITOR
  (Low Power,           (Low Power,
   High Interest)        Low Interest)
      |
Low Power
      |
Low Interest ——————————————— High Interest
```

**Manage Closely:** These people can make or break your project. Regular 1:1s, early input on decisions, no surprises.

**Keep Satisfied:** They have authority but aren't deeply involved. Periodic updates, quick escalation path when you need their support.

**Keep Informed:** They care about the outcome but can't unilaterally change direction. Regular updates, inclusion in reviews.

**Monitor:** Low-touch. Keep them in the loop but don't spend disproportionate energy.

### Influence/Impact Mapping

For each key stakeholder, document:

| Stakeholder | Role | Power | Interest | Attitude | Strategy |
|------------|------|-------|----------|----------|----------|
| [Name] | [Title] | High/Med/Low | High/Med/Low | Champion/Neutral/Skeptic | [Approach] |

**Attitude categories:**
- **Champion:** Actively supports your initiative. Leverage them.
- **Supporter:** Generally positive but not vocal. Nurture them.
- **Neutral:** No strong opinion. Educate and inform.
- **Skeptic:** Has concerns but is persuadable. Address their specific objections.
- **Blocker:** Actively opposes. Understand why and work around or through.

## Stakeholder Communication Cadence

### Communication Plan Template

| Stakeholder / Group | What They Need | Format | Frequency | Owner |
|---------------------|---------------|--------|-----------|-------|
| Executive sponsor | Strategic updates, risks, decisions | 1:1, memo | Bi-weekly | PM/Lead |
| Steering committee | Progress, budget, milestones | Deck, meeting | Monthly | PM |
| Direct team | Task-level coordination | Standup, board | Daily/weekly | Team lead |
| Cross-functional partners | Dependencies, timeline | Email, sync | Weekly/bi-weekly | PM |
| End users/customers | Impact, timeline, training | Email, webinar | Milestone-based | Product marketing |

### Rules

- Over-communicate during uncertainty (early stages, crises, pivots)
- Under-communicate when things are stable (nobody needs weekly "all good" emails)
- Match format to urgency: Slack for quick updates, email for reference, meeting for discussion
- Every stakeholder should be able to answer: "What's happening? Why? What's next?"

## Managing Up

### Framing Recommendations

**Structure for executive presentations:**
1. **Context** (30 seconds): What this is about and why it matters now
2. **Recommendation** (30 seconds): What you think we should do
3. **Evidence** (2-3 minutes): Why this is the right call
4. **Tradeoffs** (1-2 minutes): What we're giving up and why it's worth it
5. **Ask** (15 seconds): What you need from them

**Framing rules:**
- Always have a recommendation. "What do you think?" without a point of view wastes their time.
- Present 2-3 options with a clear recommendation. Execs want to choose, not generate options.
- Quantify when possible. "Saves 2 weeks" beats "saves time."
- Anticipate their questions. Prepare for "What if?" and "Why not [alternative]?"

### Pre-Wiring Decisions

**What it means:** Socializing your recommendation with key stakeholders before the formal decision meeting.

**Why it matters:** Decisions go sideways when someone raises a concern that could have been addressed in advance. Pre-wiring eliminates surprises.

**Process:**
1. Identify who has a strong opinion or veto power
2. Schedule brief 1:1s before the decision meeting
3. Share your recommendation and reasoning
4. Listen for concerns and objections
5. Adjust your proposal if the concerns have merit
6. Walk into the meeting knowing where everyone stands

**When to pre-wire:**
- Controversial or politically sensitive decisions
- Resource allocation or reprioritization
- Changes that affect multiple teams
- Anything the executive sponsor cares deeply about

## Managing Across

### Cross-Functional Alignment

**Shared goals:** Teams align when they share an outcome, not just awareness. Find the metric everyone cares about.

**Shared language:** Different functions use different words for the same thing. Establish shared definitions early.

**Shared visibility:** Use a common board, doc, or meeting where cross-functional status is visible to all.

### Conflict Resolution

**When teams disagree, diagnose the type:**

| Conflict Type | Example | Resolution Approach |
|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Data conflict** | Different teams have different numbers | Align on data source, run analysis together |
| **Priority conflict** | "Our work is more urgent" | Escalate to shared leader with impact data |
| **Resource conflict** | Both teams need the same person/system | Negotiate scope or timeline trade-offs |
| **Perspective conflict** | Engineering sees risk, product sees opportunity | Structured discussion with both views heard |
| **Values conflict** | Speed vs. quality, growth vs. sustainability | Escalate — this needs leadership alignment |

**Resolution steps:**
1. Name the conflict explicitly (most conflicts persist because they're not stated clearly)
2. Ensure each side articulates their position and their reasoning
3. Find the shared goal: "We both want [outcome] — we disagree about how"
4. Explore compromises: "What if we [partial approach] and revisit in [timeframe]?"
5. If stuck, escalate with a clear framing: "We need a decision between [A] and [B]. Here are the tradeoffs."

## Building Buy-In

### Socialization Strategy

**Phase 1: Seed the idea (weeks before)**
- Mention the problem in 1:1s and casual conversations
- Ask questions that lead people to the same conclusion
- Share data that supports the direction without proposing a solution

**Phase 2: Propose and refine (1-2 weeks before)**
- Share a draft proposal with 2-3 trusted allies
- Incorporate their feedback (and credit their contributions)
- Pre-wire with decision-makers

**Phase 3: Formal proposal**
- Present with confidence — you've already addressed the main concerns
- Acknowledge input from others: "As [Name] pointed out..."
- Ask for a specific decision, not open-ended feedback

### Evidence-Based Persuasion

| Evidence Type | When to Use | Example |
|--------------|------------|---------|
| **User data** | When the audience cares about users | "8 of 10 users couldn't complete this task" |
| **Business metrics** | When the audience cares about outcomes | "This segment generates 40% of revenue" |
| **Competitive intel** | When the audience cares about market position | "Competitors X and Y both launched this" |
| **Expert opinion** | When the audience respects authority | "Gartner recommends this approach for..." |
| **Pilot results** | When the audience is risk-averse | "We tested this with 100 users and saw..." |
| **Analogies** | When the concept is unfamiliar | "This is like how [Company] solved..." |

### Pilots and Small Wins

The most effective buy-in strategy is a small success:
1. Propose a limited pilot: "Let's try this with one team for 4 weeks"
2. Define clear success criteria: "If [metric] improves by [X%], we expand"
3. Run the pilot with high support and attention
4. Share results widely (even if mixed — honesty builds trust)
5. Use results to secure broader investment

## Influence Without Authority

### Relevant for: Consultants, embedded product people, cross-functional roles

**Build credibility first:**
- Demonstrate expertise through quality work, not claims
- Be reliable — follow through on every commitment
- Listen more than you talk in the first 30 days
- Ask good questions before proposing solutions

**Use the right currency:**
Different people are motivated by different things:

| Currency | How to Use |
|----------|-----------|
| **Knowledge** | Share useful information, industry insight, data |
| **Recognition** | Credit their contributions publicly |
| **Efficiency** | Save them time or reduce their workload |
| **Alignment** | Show how your proposal advances their goals |
| **Safety** | Reduce their risk or protect them from failure |
| **Connection** | Introduce them to people who can help them |

**Tactics:**
- Ask for advice (people who advise you become invested in your success)
- Find the informal influencers (org chart doesn't always reflect actual influence)
- Build a coalition of supporters before going to the decision-maker
- Frame proposals in terms of the other person's goals, not yours

## Navigating Organizational Politics

### Reading the Room

**Questions to answer:**
- Who actually makes decisions? (Not always the person with the title)
- Who influences the decision-maker?
- What are the unwritten rules? (How are things really done here?)
- What is each person's incentive structure? (What are they measured on?)
- What are the sensitive topics? (Past failures, ongoing tensions, personal stakes)

### Understanding Incentives

People act rationally within their incentive structure. If someone is blocking your initiative, ask:

- Does this threaten their team's resources or headcount?
- Does it imply their previous approach was wrong?
- Does it require them to change how they work?
- Does it affect their visibility or influence?
- Does it compete with their own initiative?

### Navigating Without Getting Political

- Be transparent about your goals and reasoning
- Don't talk negatively about others or their ideas behind their back
- Give credit generously — it's free and builds allies
- When someone blocks you, try to understand their perspective before working around them
- Choose battles wisely — not everything needs to be fought
- Build a reputation for fairness and follow-through — it's your most valuable asset
