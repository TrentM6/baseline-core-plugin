# Stakeholder Communication

> Shared framework for managing stakeholder relationships and tailoring communication across audiences. Applicable to strategy, product communications, project management, and GTM.

---

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Power/Interest Grid

Map every stakeholder on two dimensions to determine engagement strategy:

```
High Power
      |
  MANAGE CLOSELY        KEEP SATISFIED
  (High Power,          (High Power,
   High Interest)        Low Interest)
      |
  KEEP INFORMED         MONITOR
  (Low Power,           (Low Power,
   High Interest)        Low Interest)
      |
Low Power
      |
Low Interest ——————————————— High Interest
```

### Stakeholder Register

| Stakeholder | Role | Power | Interest | Current Attitude | Desired Attitude | Strategy |
|------------|------|-------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| [Name] | [Title] | H/M/L | H/M/L | Champion/Neutral/Skeptic | [Target] | [Approach] |

### Attitude Levels

- **Champion:** Actively advocates. Leverage them as allies and internal sellers.
- **Supporter:** Positive but passive. Ask for specific help (introductions, endorsements).
- **Neutral:** No opinion. Educate with relevant information and small wins.
- **Skeptic:** Has concerns. Address objections directly with evidence.
- **Blocker:** Actively opposes. Understand their incentives, find common ground, or escalate.

## Communication Cadence by Stakeholder Type

### Cadence Matrix

| Stakeholder Type | Format | Frequency | Content |
|-----------------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Executive sponsor** | 1:1, memo | Bi-weekly | Strategic progress, risks, decisions needed |
| **Steering committee** | Deck, meeting | Monthly | Milestones, budget, key metrics |
| **Direct team** | Standup, board | Daily/weekly | Tasks, blockers, priorities |
| **Cross-functional partners** | Sync, email | Weekly/bi-weekly | Dependencies, timeline, handoffs |
| **Customers / end users** | Email, webinar | Milestone-based | Impact, timeline, training, feedback |
| **External partners** | Call, email | Bi-weekly/monthly | Joint progress, integration status |

### Cadence Rules

- **Increase frequency** during: launches, crises, pivots, transitions
- **Decrease frequency** during: stable execution, maintenance phases
- **Every stakeholder should always know:** What's happening, why, and what's next
- **No surprises:** If news is bad, communicate it early. Don't wait until the meeting.

## Tailoring by Audience

### Depth and Framing Matrix

| Audience | Depth | Lead With | Language | Format |
|----------|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Executive / C-Suite** | Summary (3-5 points) | Business impact, risk | Revenue, growth, strategic position | One-pager, 5-slide deck |
| **Product / PM** | Moderate | User impact, product metrics | Features, user segments, funnels | Slide deck, doc |
| **Engineering** | Deep | Technical impact, tradeoffs | Systems, performance, architecture | RFC, wiki, working session |
| **Design** | Moderate | User experience, principles | Patterns, research, accessibility | Critique, Figma comments |
| **Sales / GTM** | Moderate, story-driven | Revenue impact, customer story | Deals, win rates, competitive angle | Slides, battle cards |
| **Marketing** | Moderate | Reach, engagement, positioning | Channels, campaigns, metrics | Brief, calendar |
| **Customers** | Concise | Their outcomes, their problems | Benefits, not features | Email, help doc, changelog |

### Adaptation Principles

1. **Answer their unasked question.** Execs ask "what's the status?" but mean "should I worry?" Sales asks "when does it ship?" but means "can I sell it?"
2. **Match depth to seniority.** More senior = less detail, more implications.
3. **Frame in their language.** Don't make the audience translate.
4. **Match the medium to the stakes.** Email for updates. Face-to-face for difficult conversations.

## Managing Up

### Framing Recommendations

**Structure:**
1. **Context** (30 seconds): Why this matters now
2. **Recommendation** (30 seconds): What you propose
3. **Evidence** (2-3 minutes): Data supporting the recommendation
4. **Tradeoffs** (1-2 minutes): What you're giving up
5. **Ask** (15 seconds): What you need from them

**Rules:**
- Always have a recommendation. Never go in with "what should we do?"
- Present 2-3 options with a clear recommended option
- Anticipate their top 3 questions and prepare answers
- Quantify when possible: "saves 2 weeks" beats "saves time"

### Pre-Wiring Decisions

Before any formal decision meeting:
1. Identify who has a strong opinion or veto power
2. Have brief 1:1s to share your thinking and gather input
3. Adjust your proposal based on legitimate concerns
4. Walk into the meeting knowing where everyone stands

**When to pre-wire:**
- Resource allocation or reprioritization
- Changes affecting multiple teams
- Controversial or politically sensitive decisions
- Anything the executive sponsor cares deeply about

### Escalation

**When to escalate:**
- Blocker persists more than 2 business days
- A decision is overdue and blocking work
- Scope changes will shift timeline by 20%+
- Risk has materialized and mitigation isn't working

**How to escalate:**
- Frame the problem: "[What] is blocking [impact]. We've tried [actions]. We need [specific ask]."
- Propose 2-3 options with tradeoffs
- Name the decision-maker and timeline needed
- Follow up in writing after the conversation

## Difficult Conversations

### Delivering Bad News

**Principles:**
- Fast and direct beats slow and softened
- Come with a plan, not just the problem
- Prefer face-to-face or video over email for the initial delivery
- Be prepared for silence — don't fill it with more talking
- Follow up in writing after

**Structure:**
1. State the situation clearly (no softening that creates ambiguity)
2. Explain what happened and why (without excuses)
3. Share the impact and revised plan
4. Take ownership where appropriate
5. State what you need from them

### Managing Disagreement

**When stakeholders disagree with your recommendation:**
1. Listen fully — don't defend immediately
2. Acknowledge their perspective: "I understand the concern about [X]"
3. Ask what evidence would change their mind
4. If they have valid points, adapt your recommendation
5. If you believe they're wrong, make your case with data and escalate if needed

**When stakeholders disagree with each other:**
1. Make the tradeoff explicit: "If we do X, Y is delayed. If we do Y, X waits."
2. Present data if available
3. Identify the decision-maker (RACI should clarify this)
4. Drive to a decision and document it

### Strategy Pivots

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge the current direction and the work invested
2. Share what changed (new data, market shift, customer feedback)
3. Present the new direction clearly
4. Bridge: what carries forward vs. what changes
5. Get buy-in through questions and discussion

## Async vs. Sync Communication

### When to Write (Async)

- Status updates and progress reports
- FYI information that doesn't need discussion
- Detailed analysis that benefits from careful reading
- Decisions that are made and need to be communicated
- Cross-timezone communication
- Documentation for future reference

### When to Meet (Sync)

- Sensitive or emotional topics
- Complex decisions requiring real-time discussion
- Brainstorming and creative work
- Relationship building and alignment
- When async is going in circles (3+ back-and-forth = schedule a call)

### Async Best Practices

**Messages:**
- Lead with purpose: "FYI" / "Decision needed by [date]" / "Feedback requested"
- One topic per message thread
- Use formatting for scannability (bullets, bold, sections)
- Specify response needed and deadline

**Documents:**
- State purpose and audience at the top
- Use headings aggressively — people scan before they read
- Bold the key takeaways
- End with clear next steps or a specific ask

### Sync Best Practices

- Send agenda 24+ hours in advance
- State the objective: "By end of meeting, we will [decision/alignment]"
- End with decisions, action items, and owners
- Send notes within 24 hours

## RACI for Communication

### Quick Reference

| Role | Communication Need |
|------|-------------------|
| **Responsible** | Needs task-level detail, blockers, daily coordination |
| **Accountable** | Needs progress against milestones, risks, decision points |
| **Consulted** | Needs enough context to give informed input before decisions |
| **Informed** | Needs outcomes and impacts after decisions are made |

### Common Mistakes

- Informing someone who should be consulted (they feel blindsided)
- Consulting someone who should only be informed (slows decisions)
- No one accountable for communication itself (information falls through cracks)
- Same message to everyone regardless of role (too much detail for some, too little for others)
