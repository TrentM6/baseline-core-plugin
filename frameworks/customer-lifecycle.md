# Customer Lifecycle

> Shared framework for understanding where customers are in their journey and how to engage them at each stage. Applicable to customer success, sales enablement, product marketing, and go-to-market planning.

---

## Lifecycle Stages

Every customer moves through these stages. The goal is to accelerate progression and catch regression early.

### 1. Onboarding
**Goal:** Get the customer to their first value milestone as fast as possible.

- Starts at contract signature or account creation
- Ends when the customer has experienced the core benefit of your product
- The most critical stage — early experience predicts long-term retention

**Key metric:** Time to first value (TTFV)

**Health signals:**
- Positive: Account setup complete, key features activated, first workflow completed, users invited
- Negative: Stalled setup, no logins after day 3, support tickets about basics, key stakeholders disengaged

### 2. Adoption
**Goal:** Expand usage from initial value to habitual, integrated use.

- Starts after the first value milestone
- Ends when the product is embedded in the customer's regular workflow
- The stage where customers go from "trying it" to "relying on it"

**Key metric:** Feature adoption depth, active user ratio (active users / licensed users)

**Health signals:**
- Positive: Multiple features in use, growing user count, decreasing support volume, integrations connected
- Negative: Usage plateaued, only one champion using it, features purchased but unused, workarounds instead of product usage

### 3. Retention
**Goal:** Maintain value delivery and prevent churn.

- Ongoing stage — never "complete"
- Health monitoring, regular check-ins, value reinforcement
- Where most CS effort should be focused for mature accounts

**Key metric:** Net revenue retention (NRR), renewal rate

**Health signals:**
- Positive: Consistent usage, growing usage, positive support interactions, references and referrals
- Negative: Declining usage, executive sponsor change, competitor evaluation, budget scrutiny, support escalations

### 4. Expansion
**Goal:** Grow the account through upsell, cross-sell, or increased usage.

- Only appropriate when the customer is healthy and successful
- Should feel like a natural next step, not a sales pitch
- Expansion is the strongest signal of product-market fit

**Key metric:** Expansion revenue, net revenue retention above 100%

**Health signals for readiness:**
- Product usage hitting plan limits
- Customer asking about features in higher tiers
- New teams or departments expressing interest
- Customer referencing future needs that align with your roadmap

### 5. Advocacy
**Goal:** Turn successful customers into champions who drive new business.

- The highest stage — customers actively promote your product
- Cannot be manufactured — only earned through genuine success
- The most efficient acquisition channel when it happens

**Key metric:** NPS promoters, referrals, case study participation, public testimonials

**How to cultivate:**
- Ask for referrals only after documented success
- Make it easy (provide templates, co-write case studies)
- Recognize and reward advocates
- Give them early access or input into product direction

## Customer Health Scoring

Health scoring turns subjective feelings about accounts into objective, actionable data.

### Building a Health Score

Combine multiple signal categories. Weight them based on what actually predicts churn and expansion in your business.

| Signal Category | What to Measure | Weight Guide |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Product usage** | Login frequency, feature adoption, active users | High — strongest predictor |
| **Engagement** | Meeting attendance, email responsiveness, QBR participation | Medium |
| **Support** | Ticket volume, severity, resolution satisfaction | Medium |
| **Relationship** | Executive sponsor stability, champion engagement, stakeholder access | Medium-High |
| **Outcomes** | Are they achieving their stated goals? | High — but harder to measure |
| **Contract** | Time to renewal, payment history, contract size trend | Low-Medium |

### Health Tiers

| Tier | Score Range | Meaning | Action |
|------|-------------|---------|--------|
| **Healthy** | 80-100 | Strong usage, engaged stakeholders, achieving outcomes | Maintain cadence, explore expansion |
| **Neutral** | 60-79 | Adequate but not thriving — could go either way | Investigate, increase engagement, find quick wins |
| **At Risk** | 40-59 | Declining signals — intervention needed | Escalate internally, direct outreach, recovery plan |
| **Critical** | 0-39 | Churn likely without immediate action | Executive escalation, save plan, honest conversation |

### Intervention Patterns

**When health drops one tier:** Investigate before acting. Check for data anomalies (holiday, seasonal patterns, team reorg) before triggering a response.

**When health drops two tiers:** Act immediately. Direct outreach to the champion, review recent support interactions, propose a recovery meeting.

**When health has been declining gradually over 2+ months:** This is more dangerous than a sudden drop. Gradual decline means systemic disengagement. Requires a strategic conversation, not a tactical fix.

## Churn Analysis Framework

When a customer churns, extract maximum learning.

### Categories of Churn

| Type | Description | What to Learn |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Product gap** | They needed something you don't offer | Is this a common request? Does it warrant building? |
| **Onboarding failure** | They never reached value | Where did onboarding break down? What stage? |
| **Champion loss** | Their internal advocate left or changed roles | How dependent are we on single champions? |
| **Competitive loss** | They switched to an alternative | What did the competitor offer that we didn't? |
| **Budget/priority shift** | They couldn't justify the spend | Did we demonstrate ROI clearly enough? |
| **Poor fit** | They were never the right customer | How did they get through sales? Qualify better. |

### Post-Churn Process

1. Categorize the churn type
2. Interview the customer if possible (exit interview)
3. Review the health score history — were there warning signs we missed?
4. Document the timeline: when did things start going wrong?
5. Identify the systemic lesson — what should change in our process?
6. Feed back to the relevant team (product, sales, onboarding, CS)

## Lifecycle Stage Transitions

### Successful Transitions
- **Onboarding → Adoption:** Customer hits first value milestone, begins expanding usage independently
- **Adoption → Retention:** Product is embedded in daily workflow, multiple users active, integrations in place
- **Retention → Expansion:** Customer maxing current plan, new use cases emerging, asking about additional features
- **Expansion → Advocacy:** Customer publicly credits your product, refers peers, participates in case studies

### Warning Transitions (Regression)
- **Adoption → Onboarding:** Customer tried the product but reverted to old ways. Restart with a different approach.
- **Retention → At Risk:** Usage declining, engagement dropping. Investigate and intervene.
- **Any Stage → Churn:** Analyze, learn, and decide whether a win-back attempt is appropriate.

---

## Applying This Framework

This framework is primarily used by the Customer Success skill, but it also informs:
- **Sales Enablement** — Understanding what happens after the sale helps position the product honestly
- **Product Marketing** — Lifecycle stages inform messaging for different customer segments
- **Go-to-Market Planning** — Retention and expansion are part of the revenue model
- **Research & Synthesis** — Customer health data is a research input
